guide

The syllabus, in order. A unit completes when its goal items hit their accuracy target over enough tries — that unlocks the units that depend on it. Locked units show what to clear first. Nothing here is a wall: you can always practise an open unit early.

Name the changes by ear. A progression plays on the synth; fill the roman numerals from the chips — the title & key stay hidden until you check. Pull one from the built-in collection, or paste a tab to turn any song you're learning into the same quiz.

The real-world move: find the bass first. A cadence establishes the key, then a famous bassline plays low. Name the scale degrees — the reveal tells you whose line it was.

Functional hearing in random keys, chips only. A cadence sets the key, one diatonic seventh plays: which degree is it? This is the drill the changes quiz rests on — one chord at a time.

One chord, no key. Major or minor? Seventh flavor? Root position, bass note included — the way you'd meet it in a song.

Which note is on the bottom? A chord plays with one of its tones in the bass — root position or an inversion (a slash chord). Name the bass tone. Hearing the bass under a chord is half of reading real changes, and it's what the basslines drill trains in motion.

The atoms. Root motion in a progression is just a chain of intervals — hear P4s and P5s instantly and half of chord hunting is done.

Single-note functional hearing — the melodic foundation the degrees drill (which uses chords) rests on. A cadence sets the key, the tonic sounds, then one note plays: which scale degree is it? Diatonic first; add chromatics when the seven are automatic.

Audiation — the skill every method builds on: hear it, then make it yourself with your voice. Enable the mic and sing. match a note plays a pitch to sing back; sing a degree plays a cadence and names a scale degree to produce from the key, with no reference pitch. Hold the note — it grades when you lock on.

Call & response — the productive half of ear training. An idiomatic line plays over a light comp; reproduce it on your keyboard, MIDI, or the on-screen piano. echo lets you replay it; dictation plays it once and you rebuild it from memory. Graded note-by-note. Save a phrase you like to the lick bank.

Hear a whole cadence as one gesture, not chord by chord. A tonic chord sets the key, then a short cadence plays — name the type. ii–V–I is home base; the others are the moves that make jazz turn: the backdoor, the tritone sub, the deceptive resolution.

Zoom out to the whole tune. A standard comps by at speed — listen for how the sections repeat and return, then name the form. AABA and ABAC are the 32-bar song forms; the blues is 12 bars; some tunes are a plain 16-bar or two-part AB.

Scale color — the sound of a mode, not a key. A tonic vamp anchors home, then the scale runs up. Listen for the characteristic note: lydian's raised 4th, phrygian's flat 2nd, mixolydian's flat 7th. Name the mode.

The colors on top of a chord. A seventh chord sounds, then a single extension above it, then both together — name the tension: 9 · ♭9 · ♯9 · 11 · ♯11 · ♭13 · 13. Which extensions are available depends on the chord, so the quality is a clue.

Rhythm is half of transcription and the app trained none of it. Listen to a one-bar pattern, then tap it back over a count-in — spacebar or the pad. Each hit is scored on how close it lands.

The thing that lets you play with people, and the one skill nothing here trained: feeling time. Hear a groove, then answer. Meter — count the beats per bar (a low downbeat marks each “1”): 3, 4 or 5. Feel — are the eighth-notes even (straight) or long-short (swing)?

Your lick bank — phrases you've banked from the echo tab. Play one, echo it back, or cycle it through all 12 keys playing it in each. Transposing a line into every key is how it becomes yours; each echo is graded and logged to your progress.

What actually helps you figure out chords. Naming a progression decomposes into three skills, best trained in order:

  1. Bass first. The bass is the most isolated pitch in nearly every mix, and root motion is the progression. Train it with intervals (P4/P5/m3 both ways cover most real root motion), then basslines — real songs reduced to exactly this skill.
  2. Quality second. Given the root, is the color major, minor, dominant, half-diminished? That's qualities. Triads cold before sevenths.
  3. Function last — the multiplier. Root + quality relative to the key is the roman numeral, and hearing "IV going iv" instead of "C going Cm" is what transfers between songs and keys. Train it with degrees, then apply it on real tunes with the changes quiz and the standards library.

A good session: 5 minutes of intervals to warm up, 5 of degrees, then changes or a standards transcription until your ears tire. When a tune beats you, don't reveal — replay at 0.5× and hunt the bass line degree by degree. Met a song in the wild? Grab its chords from a tab site and feed them to changes → paste a tab.

Guide-tone drill. The 3rd and 7th are what your ear latches onto — they carry the chord quality and voice-lead the changes. A ii–V–I plays with the guide-tone line on top; name whether the top note over the V chord is its 3rd or its 7th.

The built-in lead-sheet library — real tunes with melody and changes. Comp them, play the melody, or hide the chart and transcribe by ear. Transposing to a random key is the real workout.
